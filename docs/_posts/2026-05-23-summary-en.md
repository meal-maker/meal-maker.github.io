The article explains that Japanese companies' extensive diversification is driven by the combination of lifetime employment and firm-specific skills, where employees cannot be easily fired and their skills are not transferable to other firms. This analysis provides a structural explanation for why Japanese conglomerates differ from Western firms that prioritize shareholder value and focus. Understanding this model is valuable for organizational design, knowledge management, and global business strategy. The article argues that the system only makes sense when companies are insulated from outside shareholder pressure, and notes that Western firms like IBM were also highly diversified in the past. It highlights that Japanese companies hire for long-term potential and train employees in firm-specific skills rather than general occupational categories.

hackernews · d0ks · May 22, 15:22

**Background**: Lifetime employment is a practice in which large Japanese companies hire employees directly out of school and retain them until retirement, creating a stable but inflexible labor market. These firms invest heavily in on-the-job training that develops skills specific to the company's operations, rather than general skills that workers could take to other employers. As a result, companies need to redeploy employees internally when demand shifts, leading them to enter diverse business areas to keep workers employed.

Kanbots is part of a growing ecosystem of tools like Vibe Kanban and Windsurf that integrate AI agents into project management, but it distinguishes itself by being fully local and open source.

## [yt-dlp Deprecates Bun Support Over AI-Rewritten Codebase](https://github.com/yt-dlp/yt-dlp/issues/16766) ⭐️ 6.0/10

The yt-dlp project has deprecated support for the Bun JavaScript runtime, citing that Bun's AI-assisted Rust rewrite has produced a codebase that is no longer maintainable by external contributors. This decision underscores a growing tension in open source between leveraging AI for rapid code generation and the ability of human maintainers to review and sustain such code. If unmanageable AI-generated codebases become common, they could erode trust and collaboration in open ecosystem projects. Bun's Rust rewrite involves approximately one million lines of code, according to a GitHub pull request, making it infeasible for yt-dlp maintainers to fully review or update. The deprecation means future versions of yt-dlp will no longer support running on Bun.

hackernews · tamnd · May 22, 17:24

**Background**: yt-dlp is a popular open-source command-line tool for downloading videos from YouTube and other sites. Bun is a fast, all-in-one JavaScript runtime and toolkit designed as a drop-in replacement for Node.js. Recently, Bun's core developers used AI-assisted methods to rewrite large parts of its codebase into Rust, raising concerns about the maintainability and legal status of such AI-generated code in open-source projects.
